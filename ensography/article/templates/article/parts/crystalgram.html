2.2. Crystalgram Value Your Life Moments

Photography was adopted by masses after the invention of compact cameras in the beginning of XXth century; general public strives to capture moments of social and emotional value roughly since 1920. People photograph objects and scenes they consider as affirmation of their social image: cars, apparel, jewelry, delicious food and other symbols of luxury and good taste; sketchbooks, handwritten notes, books and other symbols of intellectualism and artistry.

But to capture is not enough: it is required to share with friends, relatives, colleagues, former classmates. Sharing is a way to confirm considered social value of pictured object. Instagram did a great deal to engage its users in a feedback loop of confirmation with its ‘Like’ feature. Avid instagrammers publish their pictures primarily to collect likes, and to collect followers who, again, might like future pictures.

However, the very mechanics of ‘Like’ is flawed: it creates mutual admiration society. Very often one likes photo of her friend only to receive reciprocal likes. Photo is being liked by author’s friends neither for its artistic quality nor for its social status. It is cheap to like: likes cost almost nothing and come in uncapped volumes.

Therefore, likes of Instagram can not be used as a measure of social or any other kind of value.

Let us imagine the photographic social network where to like is to pay. Even more, let us imagine that any user could set a price for her photos. Others will see only blurred contours until they pay. First person is to pay small starting fee, but for next ones price will raise similar to mechanics of bid auction. The latter will create a competitive ecosystem where it is beneficial to check your news feed frequently in order to seize low bids.

Given the limited nature of such virtual currency, users will become more picky. More valuable photos will receive more likes, and the social value of photos posted will become tangible.

We have designed The Crystalgram App to experience these ideas in greater detail. Crystalgram is the photosocial network where users have to pay crystals to view and discuss exquisite photos.

We have introduced crystals — some sort of virtual currency. Each user receives 100 crystals right after she signs up. User can gain more crystals just by browsing the ‘News Feed’ and ‘What’s Hot’ panes for a sizeable amount of time: say she will receive a crystal for each minute spent in the app.

The much quicker way to obtain crystals is to publish crystallized photos. When a follower of the user uncovers crystallized photo, he pays current uncover price. User receives 100% of crystals paid for her photos. After each payment uncover price is raised by starting bid. If the starting bid amounts to 5 crystals, then first follower will pay 5 crystals, second one — 10 crystals, third one — 15 crystals and so forth.

Crystals could be purchased via In-App Purchase, of course. If a follower wants just to spectate, he could buy 500 crystals for $0,99 or 5000 crystals for just $4,99.

Finally, any user can invest his crystals in promising photos thus making more crystals out of his prescience. For example, let a starting bid of a photo be 5 crystals. If user raises it by 10 crystals more, the starting bid will become 15 crystals. Then the next follower will pay 15 crystals: 5 will go to the author of this photo and other 10 to our lucky investor.

This monetary system allows for different user behavior:

  • an artist user could just publish her photos and receive crystals from anyone who uncovers them;
  • an idle spectator could slowly raise crystals by exploring depths of ‘News Feed’ and ‘What’s Hot’, occasionally making in-app purchases;
  • finally, a gambling connoisseur could make crystals riding the waves of trending crystallizations.

We have imagined the Crystalgram account of a typical instagrammer. She thinks each aspect of her life is important and valuable. But soon enough she will see that some of her photos raise more crystals then others. Does that correlate with social value of photos and objects they represent?
